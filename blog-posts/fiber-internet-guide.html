Fiber Optic vs. Regular Broadband: Is the Upgrade Worth It?

June 10, 2021 5 min read DreamFiber Team

The Evolution of Internet Connectivity

If your internet struggles to keep up with daily tasks, you are likely looking for an upgrade. The biggest decision you will make is whether to stick with regular broadband (copper cables) or upgrade to fiber optic internet.

At DreamFiber, we receive this question daily. Understanding the technological differences between the two will help you make the best decision for your home or business.

What is Regular Broadband (ADSL/VDSL)?

Regular broadband, often referred to as ADSL (Asymmetric Digital Subscriber Line) or VDSL, uses traditional copper telephone lines to transmit data.

  • How it works: It sends electrical signals over copper wires originally designed for voice calls.
  • Speed limits: Copper degrades the signal over long distances. The further you are from the ISP's exchange, the slower your internet.
  • Asymmetric: It offers decent download speeds but extremely low upload speeds.

What is Fiber Optic Internet (FTTH)?

Fiber to the Home (FTTH) replaces the old copper wires with ultra-thin strands of glass or plastic.

  • How it works: Data is transmitted as pulses of light rather than electrical currents.
  • Speed limits: Light travels incredibly fast and does not degrade over long distances. Speeds can easily reach 1 Gbps (1,000 Mbps) and beyond.
  • Symmetric potential: Fiber can deliver upload speeds that match your download speeds.

Key Differences: The Direct Comparison

1. Speed and Bandwidth Capacity

Regular broadband maxes out quickly, especially when multiple devices are connected. Fiber optic internet has virtually unlimited bandwidth. Whether you are downloading a 50GB video game or streaming a 4K movie, fiber handles it without bottlenecking.

2. Reliability and Weather Resistance

Copper wires are highly susceptible to environmental factors. Heavy rain, temperature fluctuations, and electromagnetic interference from power lines can cause your regular broadband to drop. Fiber optic cables do not conduct electricity, making them completely immune to weather and electromagnetic interference.

3. Latency for Gaming and Video Calls

Latency (or ping) is the time it takes for data to travel from your device to a server and back.

  • Regular broadband: Higher latency, leading to lag in online games and delays in VoIP calls.
  • Fiber optic: Near-zero latency, ensuring instant responsiveness for gamers and crystal-clear Zoom meetings.

Is the Upgrade Worth It?

If you live alone and only use the internet to check emails, regular broadband might suffice. However, the upgrade to fiber optic is absolutely worth it if:

  • You have a smart home with multiple connected devices.
  • Your family streams OTT content (Netflix, Prime, IPTV) on multiple screens.
  • You work from home and rely on cloud computing and video conferencing.
  • You are a competitive online gamer.
